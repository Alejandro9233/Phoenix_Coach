# Recovery & HRV Interpretation Rules

## Sources
- Dr. Stephen Seiler (polarized training research)
- HRV-guided training meta-analyses
- TrainingPeaks Performance Management Chart methodology

## HRV-Based Training Decisions (Overnight HRV)

### Reading Your HRV Trend
- Always look at TRENDS (3-7 day moving average), not single-day values
- A single low HRV day is NOT actionable — normal daily variation is 10-15%
- Compare against your personal baseline (currently 85 ms from COROS)
- Morning measurement is most reliable — overnight HRV from COROS is excellent

### Decision Framework
| HRV Signal | Duration | Action |
|---|---|---|
| At or above baseline | Any | Normal training — can do scheduled workout |
| 5-10% below baseline | 1 day | No action — normal variation |
| 5-10% below baseline | 2-3 days | Reduce intensity by one zone (e.g., threshold → tempo) |
| >10% below baseline | 2+ days | Reduce to Zone 1-2 only — recovery sessions |
| >15% below baseline | 2+ days | Full rest day or very light active recovery (walk, easy swim) |
| Rising trend above baseline | 3+ days | Athlete is adapting well — can push harder in quality sessions |

### Limitations (Per Seiler)
- HRV is ONE tool — combine with RHR, subjective feel, and performance data
- Highly trained athletes may show blunted HRV responses
- Poor sleep, alcohol, illness, stress ALL affect HRV independently of training
- Don't over-react: HRV supplements coaching judgment, doesn't replace it

## Resting Heart Rate (RHR) Signals

### Baseline Establishment
- Use 7-day rolling average as your baseline
- Current athlete RHR: 48 bpm (testRhr from COROS)
- Morning measurement is most reliable (or overnight from wearable)

### Decision Framework
| RHR Signal | Action |
|---|---|
| Within ±3 bpm of 7-day average | Normal — train as planned |
| 4-5 bpm above average | Caution — reduce intensity, check hydration/sleep |
| >5 bpm above average for 2+ days | Reduce to easy training, screen for illness |
| >8 bpm above average | Full rest day, consider medical check if persistent |
| Trending downward over weeks | Positive sign — improving cardiovascular fitness |
| Trending upward over weeks | Warning — accumulating fatigue, review training load |

## Training Load Management (TrainingPeaks CTL/ATL/TSB Model)

### The Three Metrics
- **CTL (Chronic Training Load) = "Fitness"**: 42-day exponentially weighted average of daily training load. Rising = getting fitter. Falling = losing fitness.
- **ATL (Acute Training Load) = "Fatigue"**: 7-day exponentially weighted average. High = you're tired. Low = you're fresh.
- **TSB (Training Stress Balance) = "Form"**: CTL minus ATL. Negative = fatigued. Positive = fresh/tapered.

### COROS Equivalents
- CTI (COROS) ≈ CTL (TrainingPeaks) — your fitness score
- ATI (COROS) ≈ ATL (TrainingPeaks) — your fatigue score
- TIB (COROS) ≈ TSB (TrainingPeaks) — your form/readiness

### Decision Framework
| TIB/TSB Value | Meaning | Action |
|---|---|---|
| +10 to +25 | Fresh, tapered | Ideal for racing or time trials |
| 0 to +10 | Recovering | Light training, preparing for quality sessions |
| -10 to 0 | Productive training | Normal training zone — this is where adaptation happens |
| -10 to -20 | Accumulating fatigue | Still acceptable in a build block, but monitor HRV/RHR |
| < -20 | Overreaching territory | Reduce load immediately, schedule deload week |
| > +25 | Undertrained/detraining | Too much rest — fitness is declining |

## Load Ratio (Acute:Chronic)
| Ratio | State | Action |
|---|---|---|
| <0.8 | Detraining risk | Increase training volume — you're doing too little |
| 0.8-1.0 | Maintenance | Stable fitness — good for recovery weeks |
| 1.0-1.3 | Optimal training zone | "Sweet spot" — building fitness safely |
| 1.3-1.5 | Functional overreaching | Acceptable for 1-2 weeks max during a hard build block |
| >1.5 | HIGH injury/illness risk | Reduce load immediately — do NOT train through this |

## Overtraining Warning Signs
When multiple of these occur together, prescribe rest:
- HRV consistently below baseline for 5+ days
- RHR elevated AND performance declining
- Athlete reports RPE >7 for sessions that normally feel moderate
- Motivation consistently below 3/5
- Sleep quality declining alongside training load increases
- Persistent muscle soreness that doesn't resolve with normal recovery
- Increased frequency of illness (colds, upper respiratory infections)
- Performance plateau or decline despite increasing training load

## Recovery Modalities
- Sleep: 7-9 hours per night — the #1 recovery tool, non-negotiable
- Nutrition: Protein within 30 min post-workout, carbs for glycogen replenishment
- Active recovery: Very easy Zone 1 swimming or walking on rest days
- Foam rolling / stretching: After training sessions, especially legs
- Hydration: Minimum 2-3 liters daily, more on heavy training days
